Abstract

The study addresses a central problem in diabetic retinopathy screening: high-performing deep learning classifiers are often difficult to interpret in a clinically meaningful way. To address this, the paper combines strong discriminative grading models with multimodal explanations. Six CNN and transformer backbones are benchmarked on APTOS 2019, multiple ensemble strategies are evaluated, Grad-CAM++ is used for visual attribution, and vision-language models generate concise textual rationales. The results show that modern CNNs provide the strongest single-model baselines, weighted soft voting yields the most consistent ensemble gains, and textual explanations can be evaluated jointly with predictive performance through explicit metrics.

Visual evidence

Grad-CAM++ overlays are used to highlight image regions contributing to the predicted diabetic retinopathy grade. The paper reports plausible but sometimes coarse localization, especially in lower grades.

Textual rationale

LLaVA and LLaVA-Med variants generate short clinician-oriented explanations conditioned on the fundus image, predicted grade, and class probabilities under conservative prompting constraints.

Quality assessment

Explanation quality is assessed using BERTScore for semantic fidelity, CLIPScore for image-text alignment, and a descriptor coverage metric for clinical completeness.
